# 019: Benchmark Credibility Analysis -- What Makes a Landmark Benchmark

**Date:** 2026-04-04
**Purpose:** Systematic analysis of how top biomedical/KG papers design and validate benchmarks, to inform ChronoMedKG-TQA design decisions.

---

## Executive Summary

After analyzing 13 major benchmarks and papers across NeurIPS D&B, Nature MI, Scientific Data, EMNLP, and ICLR, the findings are clear:

**The single most important factor is NOT human annotation -- it is external ground truth.**

Benchmarks accepted at top venues fall into a credibility hierarchy:

1. **Gold standard:** Questions from real professional exams (MedQA, MedMCQA, MMLU)
2. **Silver standard:** Expert-written questions with supporting evidence (BioASQ)
3. **Bronze standard (but accepted):** Programmatic generation from structured data, validated by cross-referencing external databases (PubMedQA-Artificial, TGB 2.0, PrimeKG)
4. **Emerging standard:** LLM-as-judge with multi-layer verification (KARMA -- NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight)

ChronoMedKG-TQA falls squarely in category 3, which is viable IF the external validation is rigorous enough. KARMA's acceptance as a Spotlight proves category 4 is now viable at NeurIPS.

## Paper-by-Paper Analysis

### 1. PrimeKG (Scientific Data 2023)

**How they validated:** Structural analysis, NOT a QA benchmark.
- Compared graph statistics (node count, edge count, connectivity) against SPOKE, HSDN, and GARD
- Showed 1-2 orders of magnitude improvement in disease coverage
- NO precision/recall against ground truth
- NO human evaluation of edge correctness
- Validation was purely quantitative: "we have more, from more sources"
- Supplemented with clinical description text from DrugBank/OMIM for multimodal analysis

**What made reviewers accept it:** Scientific Data requires a Technical Validation section but accepts structural/statistical validation for integration-type datasets. PrimeKG's value was COMPREHENSIVENESS (20 sources integrated) rather than extraction quality.

**Key lesson:** For a KG integration paper, you do NOT need to validate every edge. You need to show coverage superiority and describe provenance clearly.

---

### 2. MedQA (2021)

**Question source:** Real USMLE exam questions + mock test questions.
- English version: USMLE Step 1/2/3 questions
- Also includes Chinese and Taiwanese medical exam questions
- 1,273 test questions; each is a clinical vignette with 4-5 MCQ options

**Circularity handling:** Questions come from professional licensing exams, NOT generated by LLMs. Zero circularity risk.

**Validation:** The questions ARE the gold standard -- they were written by medical exam boards.

**What made reviewers accept it:** Real exam questions = inherent credibility. No validation needed beyond data cleaning and deduplication.

**Key lesson:** If you can anchor to an authoritative external source, the burden of validation drops dramatically.

---

### 3. BioASQ (Scientific Data 2023 for QA corpus)

**Question creation:** 21 biomedical experts wrote questions over multiple years (~500/year).
- Experts were trained in 3 physical meetings with specific annotation guidelines
- Each question includes: relevant documents, ontology concepts, RDF triples from life science triple stores, text snippets, ideal answers (summaries)
- Question types: yes/no, factoid, list, summary

**Circularity handling:** Expert-written from scratch based on real information needs. No LLM involvement.

**Validation:** Inter-annotator agreement, expert review of answers, community challenge format (annual competition).

**What made reviewers accept it:** Gold-standard expert curation with transparent methodology. The annual challenge format provides continuous community validation.

**Key lesson:** Expert curation is the platinum standard but requires significant resources (21 experts over years).

---

### 4. PubMedQA (EMNLP 2019)

**Question creation:** THREE-TIER approach:
- **PQA-L (Labeled):** 1,000 questions with expert yes/no/maybe labels
- **PQA-U (Unlabeled):** 61,200 questions derived from PubMed titles (no labels)
- **PQA-A (Artificial):** 211,300 questions with PROGRAMMATICALLY GENERATED labels

**How PQA-A was created:** Article titles phrased as questions were converted by moving copulas/auxiliary verbs. Labels were assigned automatically by matching the conclusion of the abstract to yes/no/maybe heuristics.

**Circularity handling:** Questions come from paper titles (written by authors, not LLMs). Labels come from paper conclusions.

**Validation:** The 1K expert-labeled subset validates the methodology. The artificial subset is presented as a SEPARATE resource with known limitations.

**What made reviewers accept it:** Transparent about quality tiers. The expert-labeled subset provides a ground truth anchor. The artificial subset is positioned as training data, not evaluation data.

**KEY LESSON FOR US:** PubMedQA's three-tier model is directly applicable. You can have a large programmatic set IF you have a smaller expert-validated anchor. The artificial set was accepted at EMNLP precisely because it was honest about being artificial.

---

### 5. MMLU Medical Subset (ICLR 2021)

**Question source:** Harvested from existing exam databases.
- Professional Medicine: USMLE practice questions
- College Medicine/Biology: AP exam questions
- Medical Genetics, Anatomy, Virology: College-level exam banks
- Total medical subset: ~1,300 questions across 7 subjects

**Circularity handling:** Pre-existing exam questions, not LLM-generated.

**Validation:** Minimal -- the exams themselves are validated by the institutions that created them. Hendrycks et al. did deduplication and formatting standardization.

**Key lesson:** Harvesting from authoritative sources transfers their credibility to your benchmark.

---

### 6. MedMCQA (CHIL 2022)

**Question source:** 194K questions from Indian medical entrance exams.
- AIIMS PG exam questions (1991-present) = test set
- NEET PG exam questions (2001-present) = dev set
- Mock tests + online test series by medical professionals = training set
- Each question has an explanation of the solution

**Quality control:** Levenshtein distance deduplication (threshold 0.9). Removed near-duplicates between train/dev/test.

**Circularity handling:** Real exam questions from official sources.

**Key lesson:** Scale (194K) from existing exams is achievable. The deduplication methodology is important for reviewer confidence.

---

### 7. iKraph (Nature Machine Intelligence 2025)

**How they validated:**
- NLP pipeline won 1st place in LitCoin NLP Challenge 2022 (external competition = credibility)
- Extracted information matched human expert annotations from the challenge
- Validated against 40 curated public databases (cross-database agreement)
- Random sample of 50 PubMed abstracts (1,583 entity pairs) manually checked
- Downstream validation: drug repurposing for COVID-19 (March 2020 - May 2023) with real-world outcome verification

**Circularity handling:** NLP extraction from text, validated against independent curated databases. No LLMs used for extraction (traditional NLP pipeline).

**What made reviewers accept it:** The LitCoin win + cross-database validation + real-world drug repurposing validation. Three independent validation signals.

**KEY LESSON:** Nature MI expects MULTIPLE validation signals. One is not enough. The pattern is: (1) method validation (competition/benchmark), (2) content validation (cross-database), (3) utility validation (downstream task).

---

### 8. KARMA (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight)

**How they evaluated -- THIS IS THE MOST RELEVANT PRECEDENT:**
- **LLM-Verified Correctness:** GPT-4 judges each extracted triple as likely correct/uncertain/likely incorrect. Achieved 83.1%.
- **QA Coherence (CQA):** Generated domain-specific questions answerable via KG traversal, then checked if KG-derived answers were coherent. Peak CQA = 0.617.
- **Ablation studies:** Removed individual agents to measure impact on correctness and CQA.
- **Conflict reduction:** Measured 18.6% reduction in conflicting edges through multi-layer assessment.
- **Scale:** 1,200 PubMed articles across 3 domains, 38,230 new entities.

**NO human annotation.** Zero clinician review. Accepted as NeurIPS Spotlight.

**Circularity handling:** LLM-as-judge uses a DIFFERENT LLM from the extraction LLMs. Cross-model verification reduces but does not eliminate circularity.

**What made reviewers accept it:** Novel multi-agent architecture + comprehensive ablation + LLM-as-judge now accepted as standard at NeurIPS + the QA coherence metric is clever (tests utility, not just accuracy).

**KEY LESSON FOR US:** LLM-as-judge is now FULLY ACCEPTED at NeurIPS for KG validation. KARMA had NO human annotation and got Spotlight. The key is: (1) use a different LLM as judge, (2) include ablation studies, (3) measure both accuracy AND utility.

---

### 9. TGB 2.0 (NeurIPS 2024 D&B Track)

**Benchmark design:**
- 8 temporal graph datasets across 5 domains (not just biomedical)
- Up to 53M edges
- Task: temporal link prediction (predict future edges)
- Metric: filtered Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR)
- Novel metrics: Recurrency Degree, Direct Recurrency Degree, Consecutiveness Value

**Dataset construction:** Sourced from existing timestamped databases (Wikidata, YAGO, ICEWS). NOT extracted by LLMs.

**Key finding:** Simple heuristic baselines often competitive with complex methods. Most methods fail on largest datasets.

**What made reviewers accept it:** Scale, reproducibility, public leaderboard, standardized evaluation pipeline, novel metrics that reveal properties of temporal data.

**KEY LESSON:** For a temporal benchmark, you need: (1) novel metrics that capture temporal properties, (2) demonstration that existing methods fail (motivates the benchmark), (3) scalable evaluation pipeline, (4) public leaderboard.

---

### 10. BioKGBench (ICLR 2025 submission)

**Benchmark design:**
- Three tasks: Scientific Claim Verification, KG Question Answering, KG Checking (novel)
- 2,000+ data points for atomic tasks, 225 high-quality annotated samples for agent task
- Found 96 "Refute" annotations -- errors in existing KGs verified by literature

**Validation:** Human expert annotation of 225 samples. Cross-referenced against curated databases.

**Key lesson:** Even small human-annotated subsets (225) provide credibility if the task is novel and the annotation protocol is rigorous.

---

### 11. Hetionet (eLife 2017)

**Validation approach:**
- 755 known drug-disease treatments as positive examples
- 29,044 non-treatments as negatives
- Logistic regression on 1,206 metapaths (length 2-4)
- External validation on held-out treatment sets
- Specific case study: epilepsy drug repurposing

**Key lesson:** Downstream task performance IS validation. If your KG enables a useful prediction task, that validates the KG.

---

### 12. SPOKE (Bioinformatics 2023)

**Validation approach:**
- Weekly automated rebuild from 41 databases with integrity/completeness checks
- Ontology-based structure (11 ontologies) ensures consistency
- No explicit accuracy evaluation -- validation through construction process
- 27M nodes, 53M edges

**Key lesson:** For large integration KGs, provenance + automated quality checks + ontological consistency = sufficient validation. No per-edge accuracy assessment.

---

### 13. Open Targets

**Validation approach:**
- 6 evidence types, each scored 0-1 with type-specific scoring functions
- Evidence aggregation: independent evidence for same target-disease boosts confidence
- JSON schema validation for all evidence
- Weighted scoring: GWAS upweighted, text mining downweighted
- Continuous benchmarking when new data sources are introduced
- Section-weighted document confidence (Title > Abstract > Methods)

**Key lesson:** Evidence scoring with explicit weights and transparent methodology. The scoring system itself becomes a contribution.

## Synthesis: What Makes a Benchmark Credible

### Tier 1: Absolute Requirements (paper gets desk-rejected without these)

1. **Clear provenance of every question/edge:** Where did each data point come from? Traceable to source.
2. **Transparent methodology:** Generation pipeline described in sufficient detail to reproduce.
3. **Dataset statistics:** Size, distribution across categories, difficulty distribution.
4. **Accessibility:** Data hosted on Dataverse/HuggingFace/Zenodo. Code open source. (NeurIPS 2025 can desk-reject for non-compliance.)
5. **Croissant format** (NeurIPS 2025 specifically requires machine-readable metadata).

### Tier 2: Strong Signals (what separates accept from reject)

6. **External validation against independent databases:** Cross-reference your extracted facts against Orphanet, OMIM, ClinVar, DrugBank, etc. This is the SINGLE MOST IMPACTFUL validation for programmatic benchmarks.
7. **Baseline experiments:** Run existing methods and show the benchmark is non-trivial.
8. **Error analysis:** Categorize failure modes. What types of questions are hardest?
9. **Ablation studies:** What happens if you remove components?
10. **Novel metrics:** Define metrics that capture what existing benchmarks miss (for us: temporal specificity).

### Tier 3: Differentiators (what separates good from landmark)

11. **Demonstration that existing methods fail:** "GPT-4o scores 85% on MedQA but 42% on ChronoMedKG-TQA" = irresistible narrative.
12. **Human expert ceiling:** Even a small (50-100 question) expert evaluation provides enormous credibility.
13. **Downstream utility demonstration:** Show the KG/benchmark enables something that was previously impossible.
14. **Public leaderboard:** Signals community utility and long-term impact.

## Critical Finding: The Anti-Circularity Spectrum

| Approach | Circularity Risk | Accepted at | Example |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|---------|
| Real exam questions | None | Everywhere | MedQA, MMLU, MedMCQA |
| Expert-written questions | None | Everywhere | BioASQ |
| Programmatic from structured DB | Low | EMNLP, NeurIPS D&B, Sci Data | PubMedQA-A, TGB 2.0 |
| LLM-generated + LLM-as-judge | Medium | NeurIPS 2025 | KARMA |
| LLM-generated + external DB validation | Low-Medium | Likely accepted | Our approach |
| LLM-generated + no validation | High | Rejected | - |

**Our position:** ChronoMedKG-TQA questions are generated programmatically from extracted TPPs (not directly by LLMs), but the TPPs themselves were extracted by LLMs. The validation chain is:

```
LLM extraction -> TPPs -> Programmatic question generation -> External DB validation
```

This is structurally similar to PubMedQA-Artificial + KARMA, both accepted at top venues.

## Minimum Credible Validation for Each Venue

### NeurIPS 2026 D&B Track (PRIMARY TARGET)

**Minimum requirements:**
1. Programmatic question generation from structured data (our TPPs) -- OK, no human needed
2. External database cross-validation (Orphanet, OMIM, ClinVar, DrugBank) -- CRITICAL
3. LLM-as-judge accuracy assessment (different LLM from extractors) -- now standard per KARMA
4. Baseline experiments showing temporal questions are harder than static ones
5. Dataset hosted on HuggingFace/Dataverse + Croissant metadata
6. Ablation: what happens without temporal grounding?

**Highly recommended but not strictly required:**
7. Small expert evaluation (50-100 questions reviewed by 2 clinicians)
8. Public leaderboard
9. Novel temporal-specific metrics

### Nature Machine Intelligence (STRETCH)

**Minimum requirements:**
1. Everything from NeurIPS, PLUS
2. Multiple independent validation signals (iKraph pattern: method + content + utility)
3. Downstream task demonstration (drug repurposing, diagnostic reasoning)
4. Code and data fully reproducible
5. Statistical rigor in all comparisons

**Likely required:**
6. Some form of expert validation (even small scale)
7. Comparison with multiple existing KGs (PrimeKG, iKraph, Hetionet)

### Scientific Data (BACKUP)

**Minimum requirements:**
1. Technical Validation section with quantitative metrics
2. Clear provenance documentation
3. Usage Notes section showing how to use the dataset
4. Data hosted on Harvard Dataverse or similar
5. Cross-database consistency checks

**NOT required:**
- Human evaluation (PrimeKG had none)
- Downstream task experiments
- Novel benchmark tasks

## Specific Recommendations for ChronoMedKG-TQA

### What to Do (Priority Order)

1. **External DB cross-validation (HIGHEST PRIORITY):**
   - Cross-reference temporal facts against Orphanet (disease onset ages, phenotype frequencies)
   - Cross-reference drug-disease temporal facts against FDA approval dates (DailyMed, Drugs@FDA)
   - Cross-reference gene-disease associations against ClinVar submission dates
   - Report agreement rates with confidence intervals

2. **Programmatic question generation (ALREADY PLANNED):**
   - Generate from TPP structure, NOT from LLMs directly
   - Template-based with slot filling from TPP fields
   - This avoids the LLM-circularity problem almost entirely

3. **LLM-as-judge validation (KARMA precedent):**
   - Use Claude Opus or GPT-4o (different from extraction models) to judge triple correctness
   - Report LLM-verified correctness rate
   - This is now fully accepted at NeurIPS per KARMA Spotlight

4. **Baseline experiments showing temporal gap:**
   - Run GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini on ChronoMedKG-TQA with no retrieval, static retrieval, temporal retrieval
   - The gap between static and temporal retrieval IS the paper's headline result
   - If gap is small, the benchmark (and paper) fails

5. **Small expert validation (50-100 questions):**
   - 2 clinicians reviewing 100 questions for correctness and clinical relevance
   - Report inter-rater agreement (Cohen's kappa)
   - This is NOT strictly required for NeurIPS D&B, but dramatically increases credibility
   - Can be done in 1-2 days of clinician time

6. **Novel temporal metrics:**
   - Temporal Precision: does the system get the time right?
   - Temporal Ordering Accuracy: does it correctly sequence events?
   - Stage-Specificity Score: can it distinguish what happens at stage X vs stage Y?

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT claim full human validation if you only validated a sample
- Do NOT use the same LLMs for extraction AND evaluation
- Do NOT ignore the circularity question -- address it head-on in the paper
- Do NOT generate questions that can be answered from LLM parametric knowledge alone (this defeats the purpose)

## The "Honest Numbers" Framework

Based on patterns from accepted papers:

| Metric | What to Report | Acceptable Range |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| External DB agreement | % of temporal facts matching Orphanet/FDA/ClinVar | >70% = strong, >80% = very strong |
| LLM-as-judge correctness | % judged correct by independent LLM | >75% = acceptable (KARMA: 83%) |
| Inter-annotator agreement | Cohen's kappa for expert review | >0.6 = moderate, >0.8 = strong |
| Temporal gap | Performance drop from temporal to static retrieval | >15% gap = meaningful |
| Question answerability | % of questions answerable from KG | >90% (by construction) |
| Baseline difficulty | Best LLM accuracy without retrieval | <60% = benchmark is non-trivial |

---

## Summary: The Path to Acceptance

**For NeurIPS D&B (most realistic):**
Programmatic generation from TPPs + Orphanet/FDA cross-validation + LLM-as-judge + baseline experiments + small expert review = STRONG submission. KARMA proved you don't need humans at all, but having some expert validation puts you above the bar.

**For Nature MI (stretch):**
Add multi-signal validation (iKraph pattern) + downstream task + multiple KG comparison + larger expert evaluation.

**For Scientific Data (safe fallback):**
Technical validation section with cross-database statistics + usage notes. Lowest bar but smallest impact.

**Bottom line:** The minimum viable benchmark for NeurIPS D&B does NOT require full clinician validation. It requires: (1) transparent programmatic generation, (2) external database cross-validation, (3) baseline experiments showing the benchmark is non-trivial and captures temporal reasoning that static methods miss.
